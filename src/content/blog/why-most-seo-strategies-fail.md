Most SEO strategies look reasonable on paper. They identify keyword opportunities,
outline content plans, and set ambitious traffic targets. Yet the majority fail — not
because the strategy was wrong, but because the organisation wasn't ready to execute it.

## The execution gap

The gap between strategy and results is almost always an operational one. Teams lack
clear workflows. Content production is ad-hoc. Technical fixes sit in backlogs for months.
There's no feedback loop between what's published and what's performing.

This isn't a strategy problem. It's a systems problem.

## What operational readiness looks like

Before investing in a new SEO strategy, organisations should assess whether they have:

- Clear ownership of SEO across teams
- A content production workflow that can sustain output
- Engineering capacity for technical SEO improvements
- Reporting that connects SEO activity to business outcomes
- A process for prioritising and shipping changes

## Building the foundation first

The most effective SEO work I've done has always started with operations — building
the systems, processes, and cross-functional alignment needed before any tactical
work begins. Strategy without infrastructure is just a document.

> The best SEO strategy is one your team can actually execute.

## A practical starting point

If you're planning an SEO initiative, start by mapping your current operational
capacity. Identify the bottlenecks — they're usually in process, not in knowledge.
Fix the system first, then layer on strategy.

```js
// Example: Simple SEO audit checklist
const auditChecklist = {
  technical: ['crawlability', 'indexation', 'core-web-vitals'],

  operations: ['ownership', 'workflows', 'reporting']

```

The organisations that win at SEO aren't the ones with the cleverest strategies.
They're the ones with the operational discipline to execute consistently over time.
